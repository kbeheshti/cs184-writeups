Overview:

We decided to complete part 1 and 2 of the project, since those seem the most important.Part one deals with mirrors and glass materials, while part 2 deals with microfacet materials. We now are able to create very realistic images with reflective materials, creating better illumination in our scenes.

Part 1: Mirror and Glass Materials

Our first task was to implement the refract function using snell’s law, then calculating reflections along with mirror materials, and finally refraction and glass materials using the shlick equations since they are easier.

Analysis: At a ray depth of 0, we only see our light source, since only light that does not reflect off of anything and comes directly into our camera reaches us.

At a max ray depth of 1, we see the environment around us as well as a reflection of the light source off of the balls. Additionally the light reflecting off the closer ball (now labeled the right ball) is grainy because of decreased ray depth. The balls appear black because the only light they can emit is what they reflect off of the light source, so nothing else appears to be illuminated on them.

At a max ray depth of 2, our left ball, which seems to reflect more light rays, displays the environment reflected around it, as well as the right ball, while the right ball appears to only dimly be able to reflect some of its environment around it since it is more forwards and less light strike’s its front side facing us. The left ball also appears to have a sheen on its right side from reflecting the gray back wall. Additionally the roof is black since we need more than 2 reflections to illuminate it. Our camera direction is also black since there is nothing there for light to bounce off of.

At a max ray depth of 3, the most obvious change is that the right ball appears to now look like glass and not dark, and its shadow has some light from reflection, and, on the reflection of the left ball the roof is illuminated. The reason why the right ball now isn’t dark is since light can finally bounce around to reach it, and reflect back to the camera. Additionally, the light from behind the glass ball can finally reflect and then refract through the ball and reach us, rather than reflecting like the left ball. This is evident in the bottom of the right ball where there is a concentration of light from the refraction. Additionally the reflection of the right ball on the left ball’s surface is actually still black since we don’t have enough light ray depth for it to reflect off of the surface.

At a max ray depth of 4, the reflection of the right ball on the left ball’s surface now is illuminated since we finally have the required ray depth. In terms of everything else it is mostly the same, besides a small speck of light on the right wall probably from reflection off the ground where the right ball concentrates the incoming light source’s light, or from the left ball’s reflection of the light source that was concentrated. The shadows are also noticeably softer from 2 and less harsh.

At a max ray depth of 5, there is almost no difference between a ray depth of 4 besides softer shadows and lights, and since we have a low sampling rate (to produce the image quickly and so my  computer doesn’t blow up), the lights and shadows are a bit less grainy. Additionally for every single ray depth since 2, the shadow below the balls got less intense and more matte and normal rather than stark and dark, since we have more ray bounces.

At a max ray depth of 100, the image seems like just a brighter image of max ray depth =5 with perhaps some more pronounced lighting of the walls as well as softer shadows.

Part 2: Microfacet Material

We implemented the Microfacet material to allow the realistic display of metallic materials. Here are the images with bounce of 5

At alpha = 0.005, our metallic dragon is shiny and light, and almost looks like water, since lower alpha values means more shininess.

At alpha = 0.05, our metallic dragon is more shiny and light, and actually now seems like a metallic object.

At alpha = 0.25, our metallic dragon is now more matte and non reflective like metal. Some areas of it do reflect but it doesn’t look metallic or shiny anymore.

At alpha = 0.5, our metallic dragon is now way more matte and seems to appear like non-smooth metal more than anything. The top fringe of the dragon does seem like metal, but that seems like it is due to the intensity of light coming from the light source.

Cosine hemisphere sampling is more grainy and contrasted, but it looks more shiny and the color and highlights pop more on the bunny. It seems that the cost is more and it would take a higher sampling rate for our microfacet bunny to be less grainy and not look like it was from a horror film.

Importance Sampling is far less noisy, but it looks less contrasting and has reduced brilliance. It does have this cheapening effect as compared to cosine hemisphere sampling. This could be due to the fact that we are prioritizing the rays which have a greater effect on the final image, and thus lose some of the intricacies that the image would have had.
