Overview

Project 4 was all about simulating cloth like materials using a mass and spring system to build a wireframe. Our first task was to build the wireframe, to allow for cloth-like moving like bending and shearing. Then we implement gravity into our cloth so we can complete simulations. Finally we had to stop collisions between the surface and our cloth. This project taught us how to simulate cloth physics and gravity, as well as object interactions.

Part 1: Masses and Springs

Our first task is to build a wireframe with point mass by summing over all masses to have a 1 direction mass vector, and spring structure that supports shearing forces. We also take into account vertical and horizontal cloth in our calculations by altering point mass position in xz plane, as well as when places are pinned.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

Part 2 instructs us to begin to take account force, F=ma, in our wireframe. We apply F=ma for the point masses, and then apply spring correctional forces using hooke's law to springs. We then updated the points for the point masses of the springs based on our calculations, making sure to not include our spring calculations if springs are off. We finally limit the ability for our points to change by 1.1 of rest length.

Ks determines the springiness of springs in our model, and so changing the number up or down changes how easily our springs stretch. At high numbers, our cloth is flexible and has many small wrinkles. At low numbers our cloth is rigid and its wrinkles more prominent, looking more like a sun chip or a crinkle cut lays.

Density in our model represents the number of masses per volume/area. For low density our cloth acts like silk or a shear cloth, having little bends really from pinned points. For high density our cloth sags more under its own weight and moves less.

Damping refers to the springs in our model, and describes how much energy they lose per oscillation. So a smaller damping coefficient results in far more energy staying in the cloth, with 0 never being able to stop moving, while a high damping coefficient results in a still cloth that has no movement in terms of bouncing. As we can see this is shown in our screenshots with the low damping(literally 0) resulting in a bumpy jumbled mess that won’t stop moving, while high damping results in a slow drop of the cloth, which probably shouldn’t happen, but is probably due to all the energy in the springs being lost,which almost never happens, which makes our model inaccurate. A 0 damping spring is also impossible which is why we don’t see that in real life as well, and our model works well enough for that.

Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

In this part of the project, we handle collisions with other objects. Our first object is a sphere, and here we check if any of our point masses are inside the sphere, by comparing its distance to the center of the sphere with the radius. If the distance is less than the radius, we check which direction it was going, and then change it to be outside the sphere. For planes, we just check if in the last timestep we crossed over the plane, and correct it.

Ks is the spring constant which affects the springiness of springs, so for a low ks our cloth is springy and deforms more easily, resulting in more folds around the sphere, but also an accurate representation of the sphere under the cloth for its northern hemisphere.

For a higher ks constant, of 5000, our cloth less accurately represents the sphere, but it still deforms around it.

 

For a ks of 50,000, our cloth is still and doesn’t deform around the sphere quickly or well, resulting in the stiffness and gravity defying cloth in the screenshot.

Part 4: Handling self-collisions

Self collisions: Self collisions occur when our cloth bends over itself, and goes through itself. Intersections only happen locally so we use a hashmap rather than checking every point. In this way we only have to cross check within voxels in the grid.

Ks values affect the springiness of the material, so the lower the ks value is the more the cloth folds on itself because it is springy and stretchy. The higher ks values resulted in a stiffer cloth that wouldn’t fold on itself as easily. This is seen in the 50,000 ks value one, where it is only able to fold over once and falls to the side flat. The smaller ks value folds and rumples on itself.

Density affects how heavy our cloth is and thus how fast our cloth falls as well as its bends. When we have a low density our cloth seems to curl and crumple with some resistance because there isn’t much mass or force to make it do so. But when density is high, our cloth seems to fold on itself more like silk with tighter curves. Also we noticed that at a density of 0, the cloth just floats and flies away, benign repelled by the surface.

Part 5: Shaders

A shader program can be thought of as a program that takes in data and transforms it into the pixel colors shown on the screen. Fragment shaders are a type of shader program that determines the final pixels on the screen, while vertex shaders deal with the vertices of 3d models, and together they create the lighting effects that produce the final image. The vertex shader also calculates lighting values, such as the intensity and direction of light hitting each vertex, and passes them on to the fragment shader.

The Blinn-Phong shading model is a model of lighting that takes into account all ambient, diffuse, and specular components of lighting. The ambient component represents the light that is scattered around the environment, while the diffuse component represents the light that is scattered uniformly in all directions. The specular component represents the light that is reflected in a specific direction by shiny surfaces.

To calculate the lighting, the normal vector of each surface is first calculated. This normal vector is then used to determine how much light is reflected in the diffuse and specular directions. The angle between the direction of the light source and the surface normal is also taken into account. The Blinn-Phong model uses a halfway vector, which is the direction halfway between the direction of the light source and the direction of the viewer. This halfway vector is used to calculate the specular reflection of the light, which gives the object its shininess. Overall, the Blinn-Phong shading model is a popular and widely used technique for simulating the lighting of 3D objects in computer graphics. It provides a balance between realism and efficiency, making it a versatile tool for creating high-quality visuals.

For bump maps we modify the fragment shader using surface normals from the texture. For displacement mapping we displace points that intersect the sphere. Bump mapping results in a smoother and more realistic cloth, while displacement causes small perturbations and ridges in the cloth not present in bump mapping. If you increase the displacement mapping up a lot, it eventually just looks wrong and jagged.

We also implemented the mirror shader, and for that we just reflect the eye vector over the normal of the surface to get the direction, resulting in a mirror like appearance.
